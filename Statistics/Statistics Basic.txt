						Descriptive Statistics:
Descriptive statistics involves methods for summarizing and organizing data. These methods include:

Measures of Central Tendency:

Mean: The average of all data points.
Median: The middle value when data points are ordered.
Mode: The most frequently occurring value.
Measures of Variability:

Range: The difference between the maximum and minimum values.
Variance: The average of the squared differences from the mean.
Standard Deviation: The square root of the variance, indicating data spread.
Shape of the Distribution:

Skewness: Indicates the asymmetry of the data distribution.
Kurtosis: Indicates the peakedness of the data distribution.
Graphs and Charts:

Histograms, bar charts, and pie charts are common tools to visualize data distributions and summaries.
Example:
For a dataset of exam scores: [80, 85, 90, 95, 100]

Mean: 90
Median: 90
Mode: None (all values are unique)
Range: 20
Standard Deviation: Approximately 7.91
Inferential Statistics
Inferential statistics involves methods that allow us to make conclusions or inferences about a population based on a sample of data. Key methods include:

Hypothesis Testing:

Testing claims or hypotheses about a population parameter (e.g., population mean).
Common tests: t-test, chi-square test, ANOVA.
Confidence Intervals:

A range of values used to estimate the true value of a population parameter.
Example: A 95% confidence interval for a mean might be [85, 95].
Regression Analysis:

Examines the relationship between variables.
Example: Linear regression to predict a dependent variable based on an independent variable.
Correlation:

Measures the strength and direction of the relationship between two variables.
Example: Pearson correlation coefficient.
Example:
If we have a sample mean of 90 from a sample of exam scores, we might use inferential statistics to estimate the population mean and determine if it significantly differs from a hypothesized value (e.g., 85).

Probability and Frequency Distribution
Probability
Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring and ranges from 0 to 1.

Basic Concepts:

Probability of an Event (P(E)): Number of favorable outcomes divided by the total number of possible outcomes.
Complementary Events: P(E') = 1 - P(E)
Types of Events:

Independent Events: The occurrence of one event does not affect the other.
Dependent Events: The occurrence of one event affects the other.
Probability Distributions:

Discrete Distributions: Examples include binomial and Poisson distributions.
Continuous Distributions: Examples include normal and exponential distributions.
Frequency Distribution
Frequency distribution summarizes data by showing the number of observations within each given interval.

Types of Frequency Distributions:

Absolute Frequency: The count of observations within an interval.
Relative Frequency: The proportion of observations within an interval relative to the total number of observations.
Cumulative Frequency: The accumulation of previous frequencies up to the current point.
Visual Representations:

Histograms: Show the frequency of data intervals.
Frequency Polygons: Line graphs showing the frequencies of different intervals.
Example:
For exam scores [80, 85, 85, 90, 90, 95, 100]:

Absolute Frequency: {80: 1, 85: 2, 90: 2, 95: 1, 100: 1}
Relative Frequency: {80: 1/7, 85: 2/7, 90: 2/7, 95: 1/7, 100: 1/7}
Cumulative Frequency: {80: 1, 85: 3, 90: 5, 95: 6, 100: 7}
Summary
Descriptive Statistics: Summarize and describe the main features of a dataset.
Inferential Statistics: Make inferences about populations using sample data.
Probability and Frequency Distribution: Measure and represent the likelihood and distribution of data.
